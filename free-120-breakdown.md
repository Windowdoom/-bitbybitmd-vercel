# NBME Free 120 (2026) — Question-by-Question Breakdown (119 cards)

## Q001 — PE after long flight in young woman

**Block 1 · Hematology / Coagulation · Answer: B**

**Vignette Summary:** 27F post-cross-country flight → acute dyspnea, tachycardia, hypoxia (89%), unilateral calf swelling/tenderness → PE from DVT → underlying cause.

**The Discriminator:** Young woman with unprovoked DVT/PE. Think inherited thrombophilia. Factor V Leiden is the most common.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Factor V Leiden is a point mutation (Arg506Gln) that renders activated factor V resistant to cleavage by activated protein C. Anticoagulant brake fails, thrombin generation continues unchecked, and venous stasis from prolonged immobility tips the balance to thrombosis. It is the single most prevalent heritable hypercoagulable state in people of European descent (~5%).

**Cognitive Demand:** Prevalence ranking within a shared mechanism

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Prevalence Swap. All options cause VTE; NBME forces you to pick the most common inherited cause without giving you a direct frequency clue.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: young woman on combined OCPs developing DVT (OCP + FVL = multiplicative risk). Also reappears as: recurrent spontaneous abortions in a young woman. FVL is one of the heritable thrombophilias associated with placental thrombosis. Also: post-surgical DVT in a young patient with no other risk factors.

**Variable Pivot:** If the vignette mentions heparin resistance (PTT fails to prolong with heparin) → Antithrombin III deficiency (A). If warfarin-induced skin necrosis is described at treatment initiation → Protein C deficiency (D). If the patient has mucosal bleeding instead of thrombosis → vWD or Glanzmann depending on platelet vs coagulation clues.

**Options:**

- **(A)** Antithrombin III deficiency. Real cause of unprovoked VTE but much rarer; classically presents with heparin resistance. Adjacent disease.
- **(B) ✅ CORRECT** Factor V Leiden. Most common inherited thrombophilia; APC resistance → DVT/PE, especially after immobility.
- **(C)** Glanzmann thrombasthenia. GPIIb/IIIa defect → bleeding, not clotting. Reverse mechanism.
- **(D)** Protein C deficiency. Causes VTE and warfarin skin necrosis but far less common than FVL. Adjacent.
- **(E)** von Willebrand disease. Bleeding disorder (mucosal). Reverse mechanism.

**Tid Bit:** "Five Leiden". The 5% mutation that ignores Protein C.

## Q002 — Hand deformities in middle-aged woman

**Block 1 · Rheumatology / Musculoskeletal · Answer: A**

**Vignette Summary:** 53F, 6-yr symmetric hand pain/stiffness, can't button shirt → photograph (MCP/PIP swelling, ulnar deviation, sparing DIPs) → confirmatory test.

**The Discriminator:** Symmetric small-joint arthritis with morning stiffness in a middle-aged woman = rheumatoid arthritis. Anti-CCP is the most specific serology (\>95%).

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Citrullination. Conversion of arginine to citrulline by PAD enzymes. Generates neoantigens in synovium. In genetically susceptible patients (HLA-DR4 shared epitope), anti-citrullinated peptide antibodies form, drive synovial inflammation, pannus, and erosive joint destruction. Anti-CCP outperforms RF in specificity and predicts erosive disease.

**Cognitive Demand:** Diagnostic specificity. Knowing which test confirms, not just which test is elevated

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Antibody lineup. Multiple real antibody tests offered; must know anti-CCP is the most specific for RA, not just associated.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: which antibody predicts erosive disease course in RA? → Anti-CCP (more predictive than RF). Or: woman with symmetric PIP/MCP swelling + morning stiffness > 1 hour + positive RF. Which additional test would be most specific? → Anti-CCP. Or: which antibody is more specific for RA. RF or anti-CCP? → Anti-CCP.

**Variable Pivot:** If the patient had fatigue + pruritus + elevated alkaline phosphatase → PBC → antimitochondrial antibody (B). If diarrhea + iron deficiency + dermatitis herpetiformis → celiac → HLA-DQ2 or anti-tissue transglutaminase (C). If acute migratory arthritis + facial rash (slapped cheek) in a child → parvovirus B19 (D). If the question asked about RF specifically → RF is HY but less specific than anti-CCP.

**Options:**

- **(A) ✅ CORRECT** Anti-citrullinated peptide antibody. Highly specific marker of RA; correlates with erosive disease.
- **(B)** Antimitochondrial antibody. Primary biliary cholangitis. Wrong disease.
- **(C)** HLA-DQ2 antibody assay. HLA-DQ2 is associated with celiac disease (and is a tissue type, not an antibody). Wrong territory.
- **(D)** Erythroid precursor. Anemia/parvovirus. Right fact, wrong question.
- **(E)** Thrombopoietic precursor. Platelet disorders (ITP, MPN). Wrong territory.

**Tid Bit:** Anti-CCP = "Confirms Clinically Probable" RA.

## Q003 — Medial pontine syndrome (Millard-Gubler/Foville)

**Block 1 · Neurology / Brainstem Anatomy · Answer: B**

**Vignette Summary:** 80F post-stroke. Right gaze intact. Left gaze: right eye adducts but left eye stays midline (left abduction failure). Right hemiparesis plus right vibration loss. Lesion in left medial pons involving abducens nucleus/fascicle and corticospinal tract.

**The Discriminator:** Failure of left eye abduction (not adduction) plus right hemiparesis equals left medial pontine syndrome. This is CN VI (abducens) palsy, not internuclear ophthalmoplegia. INO is adduction failure from MLF damage. Abduction failure is CN VI.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** A paramedian pontine infarct involving the abducens nucleus and the corticospinal tract also damages the genu of the facial nerve as it loops around CN VI. This produces an ipsilateral (left) lower motor neuron facial palsy, an asymmetric smile, combined with contralateral hemiparesis. This is the Millard-Gubler/Foville pattern.

**Cognitive Demand:** Anatomical localization to a specific brainstem level and structure

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Level confusion. All wrong answers are real cranial nerve deficits, but each belongs to a different brainstem level. NBME tests whether you know which structures co-localize.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: any brainstem stroke with a combination of ipsilateral CN palsy + contralateral hemiparesis (crossed syndrome) → localize the lesion level by which CN is involved. Pons = CN V, VI, VII. Midbrain = CN III, IV. Medulla = CN IX, X, XI, XII. Also reappears as: internuclear ophthalmoplegia questions (MLF lesion).

**Variable Pivot:** If the lesion caused a down-and-out eye with ptosis and blown pupil + contralateral hemiparesis → midbrain, Weber syndrome, CN III. If the lesion caused ipsilateral facial anesthesia + contralateral body pain/temperature loss + hoarseness + Horner → lateral medulla, Wallenberg, PICA. If the lesion caused tongue deviation + contralateral hemiparesis + contralateral proprioception loss → medial medulla, CN XII.

**Options:**

- **(A)** Left facial anesthesia. Trigeminal sensory (CN V) is mid-pons lateral; not the territory here. Wrong territory.
- **(B) ✅ CORRECT** Asymmetric smile. CN VII fascicle wraps the abducens nucleus; both fall together.
- **(C)** Hoarseness. CN X (medulla). Wrong level.
- **(D)** Loss of accommodation. CN III/midbrain. Wrong level.
- **(E)** Tongue paralysis. CN XII (medial medulla). Wrong level.

**Tid Bit:** Pons CN VI + VII travel together. Lose lateral gaze, lose the smile.

## Q004 — Pharyngitis with pancytopenia, heterophile-negative

**Block 1 · Infectious Disease / HIV · Answer: C**

**Vignette Summary:** 32M, 2 wk fever/sore throat, generalized lymphadenopathy, tonsillar exudate, pancytopenia, negative monospot → acute retroviral syndrome.

**The Discriminator:** Mononucleosis-like illness + pancytopenia + heterophile-negative = acute HIV seroconversion.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Primary HIV infection produces a high plasma viremia with massive CD4 T-cell infection in lymphoid tissue, leading to fever, generalized lymphadenopathy, pharyngitis, and transient cytopenias (marrow suppression by cytokines and direct viral effect). Heterophile (Monospot) is negative because the antibody response is HIV-specific, not EBV-driven.

**Cognitive Demand:** Differential diagnosis of heterophile-negative mononucleosis syndrome

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Negative Monospot anchor. Most students eliminate HIV because they default to EBV for mononucleosis-like illness. The negative heterophile antibody is the pivot that forces you away from EBV.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: young person with fever/rash/lymphadenopathy + negative monospot → order HIV RNA viral load. Also: which test is most sensitive for acute HIV infection? → HIV RNA PCR (p24 antigen or antibody test may be negative in window period). Also: patient 2-4 weeks after high-risk exposure with flu-like illness → acute retroviral syndrome.

**Variable Pivot:** If heterophile antibody test is positive → EBV (A). If the patient had atypical lymphocytosis WITHOUT pancytopenia + posterior cervical lymphadenopathy → still EBV (A). If patient had genital ulcer preceding the lymphadenopathy → LGV (D). If patient had sudden sore throat + fever + anterior cervical nodes ONLY + WBC elevated → Strep (E).

**Options:**

- **(A)** EBV. Classic mono, but heterophile is positive in \>85%. Half-truth.
- **(B)** Gonococcal pharyngitis. Usually mild/asymptomatic; no pancytopenia. Wrong fit.
- **(C) ✅ CORRECT** HIV. Acute retroviral syndrome = mono-like with cytopenias, heterophile negative.
- **(D)** LGV (Chlamydia L1-L3). Inguinal buboes and genital ulcer, not pharyngitis with pancytopenia. Wrong disease.
- **(E)** Streptococcal pharyngitis. Exudative tonsillitis but no diffuse lymphadenopathy or pancytopenia. Adjacent.

**Tid Bit:** Mono symptoms + Monospot negative = check HIV.

## Q005 — AKI on oxacillin with rash + eosinophils

**Block 1 · Nephrology / Drug-Induced AKI · Answer: C**

**Vignette Summary:** 60F, 3 wk into IV oxacillin for endocarditis → fever, rash, AKI (Cr 5.7), peripheral eosinophilia, urine eosinophils and WBC casts → acute interstitial nephritis.

**The Discriminator:** Drug + rash + eosinophils + WBC casts = AIN. Penicillins (esp. Methicillin/oxacillin) are textbook offenders.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** AIN is a type IV (and partly type I) hypersensitivity reaction in which the drug or its metabolite haptenizes tubular antigens. T cells and eosinophils infiltrate the renal interstitium, producing edema, tubulitis, and impaired concentrating/reabsorptive function. Hyperkalemia and acidosis follow tubular dysfunction.

**Cognitive Demand:** Histopathological pattern identification matched to clinical presentation

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** AKI type identification. Multiple causes of AKI are offered; the urinalysis (WBC casts + eosinophils) is the definitive clue that places this in the interstitium, not the glomerulus or tubule.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: NSAIDs causing AKI with rash and eosinophils. Or: PPI-induced AIN (increasingly tested. Often no rash, just unexplained AKI on PPI). Or: rifampin causing AIN. The triad of fever + rash + eosinophilia is classic but appears in only 10-30% of real cases. WBC casts with eosinophiluria is more reliable.

**Variable Pivot:** If muddy brown granular casts instead of WBC casts → ATN. If red blood cell casts + hypertension + hematuria → crescentic GN (B). If massive proteinuria + hypoalbuminemia + edema → nephrotic syndrome, think FSGS or membranous nephropathy. If normal urinalysis + high BUN/Cr after contrast or surgery → contrast nephropathy (ATN variant).

**Options:**

- **(A)** Collapsing FSGS. HIV-associated nephropathy; nephrotic, no eosinophils. Wrong disease.
- **(B)** Glomerular hypertrophy with hemorrhage/necrosis. Crescentic/RPGN picture; would show RBC casts, not eos. Wrong territory.
- **(C) ✅ CORRECT** Interstitial inflammatory infiltrate. Classic AIN histology. Lymphocytes and eosinophils in interstitium.
- **(D)** Mesangial expansion + GBM thickening. Diabetic nephropathy. Wrong tempo.
- **(E)** Proximal tubular dilation, brush border loss. ATN; would show muddy brown casts, no eos. Adjacent.

**Tid Bit:** Drug + rash + WBC casts with eosinophils = AIN. Stop the drug.

## Q006 — Cervical syrinx + headache → Chiari

**Block 1 · Neurology / Spinal Cord · Answer: D**

**Vignette Summary:** 20F, progressive arm weakness + cape-like pinprick loss → MRI: cervical syringomyelia + back pain/headache → ask about what?

**The Discriminator:** Acquired syringomyelia in young adults is most often post-traumatic; congenital is from Chiari I. The headache (occipital, valsalva) plus syrinx points to Chiari, but the question asks what history to obtain. Trauma is the classic acquired cause that can be missed.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Spinal cord trauma can obstruct CSF flow and create a fluid-filled cavity (syrinx) that expands over months to years. It enlarges centrally, destroying the crossing spinothalamic fibers in the anterior white commissure → cape-like loss of pain/temperature with sparing of dorsal columns initially.

**Cognitive Demand:** Etiology recognition for an acquired structural lesion

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Chiari vs trauma misdirect. The clinical picture (headache + syrinx) could suggest Chiari I, but the question asks what history to obtain, and trauma is the most common acquired cause that a patient would not volunteer.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: athlete with progressively worsening hand weakness and cape-like sensory loss after a collision sport → post-traumatic syringomyelia (spinal cord injury years before symptoms). Also: patient with Chiari I malformation (occipital headache that worsens with Valsalva) + cervical syrinx → congenital version.

**Variable Pivot:** If the MRI also showed cerebellar tonsillar herniation through the foramen magnum → Chiari I malformation (congenital; different history needed. Birth history, developmental issues). If MRI showed an enhancing mass within the syrinx → intramedullary tumor (weight loss becomes relevant, E). If the patient had prior meningitis → post-inflammatory CSF obstruction.

**Options:**

- **(A)** Diet. Not linked to syrinx formation. Irrelevant.
- **(B)** Family illness. Syringomyelia is not Mendelian. Irrelevant.
- **(C)** Recent travel. Infections (e.g., cysticercosis) rarely cause syrinx; not first-line history. Adjacent.
- **(D) ✅ CORRECT** Trauma. Most common acquired cause of syringomyelia.
- **(E)** Weight loss. Implies malignancy, but intramedullary tumor would show as a mass, not classic central syrinx. Lower yield.

**Tid Bit:** Syrinx = "cape" pain/temp loss; ask about old neck injury.

## Q007 — Intermittent claudication in diabetic

**Block 1 · Cardiology / Vascular Medicine · Answer: E**

**Vignette Summary:** 68M, CAD + DM + dyslipidemia → bilateral calf pain with walking, relieved by rest, hairless atrophic cool feet, absent pedal pulses → peripheral arterial disease.

**The Discriminator:** Classic intermittent claudication in an atherosclerotic patient = atherosclerosis of lower-extremity arteries.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Atherosclerosis begins with endothelial injury and LDL infiltration into the intima. Macrophages take up oxidized LDL forming foam cells; smooth muscle migrates and a fibrous cap forms over a lipid-rich necrotic core. Luminal narrowing limits flow reserve; exercise demand exceeds supply → ischemic muscle pain that resolves with rest.

**Cognitive Demand:** Mechanism identification in atherosclerotic large-vessel disease

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Vasculitis vs atherosclerosis. Several options describe real vascular pathologies; must match the demographic (older diabetic male with multiple CV risk factors) to the correct mechanism.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: which test confirms PAD? → Ankle-brachial index (ABI) < 0.9. Or: management of claudication → supervised exercise program first, then cilostazol, then revascularization. Or: what is the mechanism of cilostazol? → phosphodiesterase-3 inhibitor → increased cAMP → vasodilation + anti-platelet.

**Variable Pivot:** If young woman + resistant hypertension + renal bruit → fibromuscular dysplasia of renal artery (A). If > 50 years old + temporal headache + jaw claudication + high ESR → giant cell arteritis (B). If young male smoker + digital ulcers + no other CV risk factors → Buerger disease (D). If small-vessel disease in a chronic hypertensive → hyaline arteriosclerosis (C).

**Options:**

- **(A)** Fibromuscular dysplasia. Young women, renal artery "string of beads." Wrong demographic.
- **(B)** Giant cell arteritis. Temporal artery, jaw claudication, age \>50 but vision/ headache, not legs. Wrong vessel.
- **(C)** Hyaline arteriosclerosis. Small-vessel HTN/DM change in arterioles (kidney), doesn't cause claudication. Wrong size vessel.
- **(D)** Segmental inflammation of medium vessels. Buerger disease (young smoker) or PAN. Wrong demographic.
- **(E) ✅ CORRECT** Vascular obstruction by lipid-rich plaques. Atherosclerotic PAD.

**Tid Bit:** "Hair-less, pulseless, cool" leg with exertional pain = atheromatous PAD.

## Q008 — Smoking cessation counseling

**Block 1 · Behavioral Science / Communication · Answer: A**

**Vignette Summary:** 58M with COPD, not ready to quit smoking → wife coughs from smoke → best physician response.

**The Discriminator:** Motivational interviewing for a precontemplation/contemplation patient: elicit the patient's own reasons for change (autonomy support), do not lecture or shame.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Open-ended, ambivalence-exploring questions help the patient articulate intrinsic motivators, increasing readiness to change. Confrontational, guilt-based, or fact-dumping statements provoke resistance and reduce the likelihood of quitting.

**Cognitive Demand:** Application of motivational interviewing technique

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Righting reflex. Physicians instinctively want to give advice or guilt-trip the patient into changing. NBME tests whether you recognize the non-confrontational, exploratory approach of MI.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as any pre-contemplative patient (alcohol, drug use, diet, exercise). The correct MI opening is always an open-ended question exploring ambivalence or values, not advice, guilt, or confrontation. The stages are pre-contemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance.

**Variable Pivot:** If the patient were in contemplation ("I've been thinking about quitting") → explore ambivalence specifically, build on self-efficacy. If patient is in preparation ("I want to quit, what should I do?") → prescribe varenicline, NRT, bupropion and set a quit date. If patient refuses a clearly beneficial intervention and you need to assess capacity → assess cognition and decision-making ability.

**Options:**

- **(A) ✅ CORRECT** "Are there any reasons you might want to quit?". Open- ended motivational interviewing prompt.
- **(B)** "Are you aware your lung condition is chronic?". Lecturing/scare tactic; doesn't elicit change talk.
- **(C)** "I'm sure you don't want your wife to suffer…". Guilt-tripping; damages alliance.
- **(D)** "Most of your health issues would improve…". Unsolicited advice; closed message.
- **(E)** "Why haven't you been able to stay off cigarettes?". Confrontational/ blaming; raises defensiveness.

**Tid Bit:** MI mantra. Explore ambivalence, don't argue for change.

## Q009 — Failure-to-thrive toddler on excess formula

**Block 1 · Pediatrics / Nutrition · Answer: B**

**Vignette Summary:** 18-mo girl, was 25th %ile for both, now \<5th %ile weight, length intact, mid-parental height normal. Diet = two 8-oz bottles of formula + small table food → thin, otherwise normal.

**The Discriminator:** Drop in weight with preserved length = inadequate caloric intake. At 18 months she should be on whole milk + a varied table-food diet; formula alone is too low calorie/volume for her needs and she's eating little real food.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Energy intake below metabolic demand depletes fat stores first (weight drops), then linear growth (length); finally head circumference. Her length and parental heights argue against endocrine or genetic causes. She has nutritional FTT.

**Cognitive Demand:** Growth pattern interpretation distinguishing organic from non-organic causes

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Weight-height discordance. Weight falling off while height is maintained means something is consuming calories without affecting linear growth. Organic causes typically affect both; caloric insufficiency hits weight first.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: any infant on excessive cow's milk formula displacing solid foods → iron deficiency + caloric insufficiency. Cow's milk at 18 months should be limited to 16-24 oz/day with solid food diet. Or: toddler with isolated low weight → food diary and feeding observation before labeling organic disease.

**Variable Pivot:** If BOTH height and weight fell below 5th percentile proportionally → think constitutional delay (C) or familial short stature (D) based on parental heights. If the child had vomiting + fatty stools + failure to thrive → cystic fibrosis or celiac disease. If electrolyte abnormalities + hyperpigmentation → adrenal insufficiency (A).

**Options:**

- **(A)** Adrenal insufficiency. Would show hypotension, hyperpigmentation, hyponatremia. Absent.
- **(B) ✅ CORRECT** Caloric insufficiency. Inadequate solids → weight loss while height preserved.
- **(C)** Constitutional growth delay. Delays both height and bone age; usually shows in older children. Wrong tempo.
- **(D)** Familial short stature. Parents are average/tall; height is normal anyway. Doesn't fit.
- **(E)** GH deficiency. Would impair linear growth predominantly. Reverse pattern.

**Tid Bit:** Weight drops before height. Think calories first.

## Q010 — Adding yogurt to a food-frequency questionnaire

**Block 1 · Biostatistics / Research Methods · Answer: A**

**Vignette Summary:** Prospective cohort of dietary calcium vs osteoporosis. FFQ initially missing yogurt → adding it improves what?

**The Discriminator:** Missing a major calcium source = systematic measurement error (misclassification of exposure). Fixing it improves accuracy of the exposure measure, i.e., internal validity.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Internal validity is the degree to which a study correctly measures what it intends to measure within its sample. Omitting yogurt would systematically underestimate calcium intake, biasing the calcium-osteoporosis association toward (or away from) the null. Including yogurt reduces measurement bias and strengthens internal validity.

**Cognitive Demand:** Identify which research quality metric is improved by adding a missing exposure variable

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Reliability vs validity confusion. Students confuse internal reliability (consistency) with internal validity (accuracy of measurement). Adding a food item doesn't change consistency; it changes completeness of measurement.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: which quality of a diagnostic test does adding a new lab value improve? → If the new value helps it measure the true disease state better → validity. Or: screening test misses cases because a key symptom was excluded from the criteria → internal validity. Missing a confounder → threatens internal validity of conclusions.

**Variable Pivot:** If two raters score the same questionnaire differently → inter-rater reliability (B). If participants are dropping out → response rate (C). If the significance threshold is being adjusted for multiple comparisons → Type I error (D). If a key confounding variable (like smoking status) was not measured → this is a threat to internal validity in a different way.

**Options:**

- **(A) ✅ CORRECT** Internal validity. Better exposure capture = less measurement bias.
- **(B)** Inter-rater reliability. Concerns agreement between observers; FFQ is self-report. Wrong concept.
- **(C)** Response rate. Depends on willingness to participate, not item content.
- **(D)** Type I error. α is set by the investigator; questionnaire content doesn't change it.

**Tid Bit:** Accurate exposure measurement = internal validity.

## Q011 — Chagas screening positive

**Block 1 · Infectious Disease / Parasitology · Answer: D**

**Vignette Summary:** 40F from El Salvador, asymptomatic, donor blood positive for T. Cruzi → vector?

**The Discriminator:** Trypanosoma cruzi is transmitted by the reduviid ("kissing") bug, which defecates near the bite; the parasite enters when the host rubs feces into the wound.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Reduviid bugs (Triatoma) feed at night around the face; their feces contain trypomastigotes that penetrate mucosa or broken skin. Acute infection may show a Romaña sign; chronic disease causes cardiomyopathy and megacolon/megaesophagus years later. She's in the indeterminate phase.

**Cognitive Demand:** Vector-pathogen pairing for a specific parasitic infection

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Latin America + immigration → Chagas should fire immediately, but students who don't know the reduviid bug will guess mosquito (too familiar).

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: Central/South American immigrant with dilated cardiomyopathy or arrhythmia + achalasia/megacolon → Chagas disease. Or: which drug treats Chagas? → Benznidazole or nifurtimox. Or: describe the mechanism of cardiac disease in Chagas → T. Cruzi destroys autonomic ganglia of the heart and GI tract.

**Variable Pivot:** If mosquito vector + cyclic fevers + ring forms on blood smear → malaria. If black fly + subcutaneous nodules + vision loss → onchocerciasis. If tick + bull's-eye rash → Lyme disease. If tick + petechial rash starting on wrists and ankles → Rocky Mountain spotted fever.

**Options:**

- **(A)** Bedbug. Cimex; nuisance bites, no systemic disease.
- **(B)** Black fly. Onchocerca volvulus (river blindness). Wrong parasite.
- **(C)** Mosquito. Malaria, filariasis, arboviruses. Wrong parasite.
- **(D) ✅ CORRECT** Reduviid bug. Vector of T. Cruzi.
- **(E)** Tick. Lyme, RMSF, babesia. Wrong vector.

**Tid Bit:** Kissing bug poops the parasite. You scratch it in.

## Q012 — Patient refusing meds with herbal tea

**Block 1 · Behavioral Science / Patient Communication · Answer: A**

**Vignette Summary:** 40M, recurrent IBS visits, drinks grandmother's herbal tea, refuses medication → best response.

**The Discriminator:** Unclear agenda. The patient keeps coming back yet refuses standard therapy. Use an open-ended question to clarify his expectations and values without judgment.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** Patient-centered interviewing recommends eliciting the patient's ideas, concerns, and expectations early. Asking what he hopes you can do opens dialogue, respects autonomy, and uncovers his goals (symptom validation? exclude serious disease? endorse his tea?).

**Cognitive Demand:** Therapeutic communication with a patient who uses alternative medicine and refuses conventional treatment

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** The urge to educate or convince. Most wrong answers either push medication or express frustration. The correct answer explores what the patient actually wants from the visit.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as any patient who refuses standard treatment (Jehovah's Witness refusing blood, patient refusing chemotherapy, patient using alternative medicine). The correct first response is always to explore goals and understanding. Not to convince, guilt, threaten, or abandon.

**Variable Pivot:** If the patient refused treatment AND appeared to lack decision-making capacity → assess capacity first. If the patient were a minor → parents' decisions + child's best interest analysis. If the patient had a red-flag symptom (rectal bleeding, weight loss) suggesting something dangerous → address the red flag directly before exploring communication preferences.

**Options:**

- **(A) ✅ CORRECT** "Help me understand what you are hoping I can do for you today.". Open-ended, patient-centered.
- **(B)** "I'd like to prescribe medication if you'd reconsider…". Pushes agenda before understanding his.
- **(C)** "I'm not sure why you keep coming to see me.". Dismissive; ruptures alliance.
- **(D)** "Let's talk about the herbal tea…". Narrows prematurely.
- **(E)** "There's nothing I can do if you refuse medication.". Confrontational and false.

**Tid Bit:** When confused about the visit, ask the patient what success looks like.

## Q013 — BPH pharmacotherapy

**Block 1 · Urology / Pharmacology · Answer: B**

**Vignette Summary:** 72M, frequency, nocturia, weak stream, symmetric enlarged prostate without nodules → benign prostatic hyperplasia → drug mechanism?

**The Discriminator:** First-line symptomatic BPH therapy = α1-adrenergic antagonist (tamsulosin, terazosin).

**Why the Answer is Correct:** α1A receptors predominate on prostatic smooth muscle and bladder neck. Blocking them relaxes the dynamic component of bladder outlet obstruction, improving urinary flow within days. (5α- reductase inhibitors shrink the gland over months but weren't an option.)

**Cognitive Demand:** Receptor pharmacology. Correct receptor type and correct agonist/antagonist direction

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Alpha/beta + agonist/antagonist matrix. Four possible combinations on alpha, four on beta. Must know BPH uses alpha-1 antagonism specifically, not just "alpha blocker."

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: which drug class should be avoided in BPH? → Alpha-1 agonists (decongestants like pseudoephedrine) worsen obstruction. Or: man with BPH + hypertension → use an alpha-1 blocker (doxazosin, terazosin) to treat both simultaneously. Or: 5-alpha reductase inhibitor vs alpha-1 blocker. Which works faster? → Alpha-1 blocker (days vs months for 5-ARI).

**Variable Pivot:** If the question asks about shrinking prostate gland size → 5-alpha reductase inhibitor, finasteride (blocks DHT). If the prostate has a hard irregular nodule → biopsy before treating for BPH. If overactive bladder (urgency/frequency without obstruction) → antimuscarinic (oxybutynin) or beta-3 agonist (mirabegron).

**Options:**

- **(A)** α1 agonism. Would worsen obstruction. Reverse mechanism.
- **(B) ✅ CORRECT** α1 antagonism. Relaxes prostatic/bladder neck smooth muscle.
- **(C)** α2 agonism. Clonidine; lowers central sympathetic tone, not BPH.
- **(D)** α2 antagonism. Mirtazapine effect on mood; irrelevant.
- **(E)** β1 agonism. Cardiac inotropy. Wrong system.
- **(F)** β1 antagonism. Antihypertensive; no urinary benefit.

**Tid Bit:** "-osin" relaxes the prostate; "-asteride" shrinks it.

## Q014 — Acute mania with psychotic features

**Block 1 · Psychiatry / Diagnostic Classification · Answer: A**

**Vignette Summary:** 33F, 2-mo grandiose delusion (married to president's twin), decreased sleep, pressured speech, loose associations, auditory hallucinations of "husband" → diagnosis.

**The Discriminator:** Distractibility, decreased need for sleep, grandiosity, pressured speech, and goal- directed behavior = manic episode. Psychotic features (delusions, hallucinations) are mood-congruent.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** DSM bipolar I manic episode requires ≥1 week of elevated/irritable mood plus ≥3 DIGFAST symptoms (Distractibility, Impulsivity, Grandiosity, Flight of ideas, Activity, Sleep decreased, Talkativeness). Psychotic features that occur exclusively during mood episodes still classify as bipolar with psychotic features. Not schizophrenia.

**Cognitive Demand:** Differentiating manic psychosis from primary psychotic disorders based on phenomenology

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Schizophrenia anchoring. The presence of hallucinations and delusions makes students immediately think schizophrenia. The manic features (decreased sleep, pressured speech, grandiosity, goal-directed behavior) are the discriminating elements.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: psychotic symptoms + prominent mood features → if mania, Bipolar I with psychotic features; if depression, MDD with psychotic features. **Costume Change:** Reappears as: power calculation questions. What determines power? Sample size + effect size + alpha level + variability. Or: study with negative result. Could be true negative OR underpowered (Type II error). Or: increasing alpha to 0.1 → decreases Type II error but increases Type I error.

**Variable Pivot:** If the investigators changed alpha from 0.05 to 0.01 → decreased Type I error, increased Type II error. If the effect size were larger → same sample size, more power, less Type II error. If the study used one-tailed vs two-tailed test → one-tailed has more power but higher risk of missing effect in opposite direction.

**Options:**

- **(A)** Greater Type I. α is fixed by threshold, not affected by n.
- **(B)** Greater Type II. Opposite of truth.

- **(D) ✅ CORRECT** Less Type II. More power → fewer false negatives.

## Q112 — Normal distribution. 50th percentile

**Block 6 · Biostatistics / Normal Distribution · Answer: D**

**Vignette Summary:** Mean leukocyte count 7500/mm3, SD 1000/mm3, gaussian distribution → which range contains approximately 50% of individuals?

**The Discriminator:** In a normal distribution, the mean equals the median. By definition, exactly 50% of all values fall below the mean. No range arithmetic needed. Half the population is below 7500.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** A gaussian distribution is perfectly symmetric around its mean. The median (the value that splits the population exactly in half) equals the mean. Therefore exactly 50% of individuals have a leukocyte count less than 7500/mm3. This is not an approximation. It is the definition of the median in a symmetric distribution.

**Cognitive Demand:** Calculate the percentage of values below the mean in a normal distribution

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** In a perfect normal (Gaussian) distribution, the MEAN equals the MEDIAN equals the MODE. Therefore, exactly 50% of values fall below the mean. By definition.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: properties of the normal distribution. Mean = median = mode. 68-95-99.7 rule for ±1, ±2, ±3 SDs. Skewed distributions: if positively skewed → mean > median > mode. If negatively skewed → mode > median > mean. The median is always more robust to outliers than the mean.

**Variable Pivot:** If asked what percentage falls between 6500-8500 (mean ±1 SD) → 68%. If asked what percentage falls above the mean → 50%. If the distribution were positively skewed → mean would be pulled higher than the median, so > 50% would fall below the mean. If asked about the 95th percentile → approximately mean + 1.65 SD.

**Options:**

- **(A)** 5500-9500. This is mean plus or minus 2 SD, capturing approximately 95% of the population. Far too wide for 50%.
- **(B)** Less than 6500 or greater than 8500. These are the tails outside plus or minus 1 SD, containing approximately 32% of the population. Not 50%.
- **(C)** 6500-8500. This is mean plus or minus 1 SD, capturing approximately 68% of the population. Common trap. 68 is not 50.
- **(D) ✅ CORRECT** Less than 7500. CORRECT. Exactly 50% of a symmetric distribution falls below the mean. The mean IS the median.
- **(E)** Greater than 9500. Only approximately 2.5% of the population lies here (beyond +2 SD).

**Tid Bit:** Mean = median in a normal distribution. Half the population is always below the mean. Do not confuse 68% (plus or minus 1 SD) with 50%.

Atypical femur fractures are an oversuppression injury, not a lytic injury. The myeloma is the reason the patient is on the drug, not the reason for the fracture.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: postmenopausal woman on alendronate for 8 years presents with thigh pain and a transverse midshaft fracture (same answer). Reappears as: bisphosphonate patient with jaw pain after a tooth extraction (osteonecrosis of the jaw, same drug-class). Reappears as: zoledronate patient with perioral tingling shortly after infusion (transient hypocalcemia). All three are bisphosphonate adverse effects on different organ systems.

**Variable Pivot:** If the question gives a focal punched-out lytic lesion on skull or vertebra plus hypercalcemia plus suppressed PTH in a myeloma patient, that is the RANKL-driven lytic mechanism (a different mechanism, different question). If the question gives chronic vitamin D deficiency plus pseudofractures plus elevated alkaline phosphatase, that is osteomalacia. The discriminator here is the DRUG, not the disease behind it.

**Options:**

- **(A) ✅ CORRECT** Bone remodeling. Oversuppressed remodeling → accumulated microdamage → atypical fracture.
- **(B)** Osteoblast generation. Bisphosphonates target osteoclasts, not osteoblast genesis.
- **(C)** Serum calcium. Typically normal or low-normal; not the lesion mechanism.
- **(D)** 25-OH vitamin D. Not altered by bisphosphonate use.
- **(E)** PTH. Unchanged; would not produce a midshaft transverse fracture pattern.

**Tid Bit:** Atypical femur fracture (atraumatic, transverse, subtrochanteric or midshaft) on long-term bisphosphonate is an OVERSUPPRESSION injury. Microdamage cannot be cleared. The fix is a drug holiday plus intramedullary nailing. Same drug class also causes ONJ and pill esophagitis.

## Q118 — Lateral ankle sprain

**Block 6 · Orthopedics / Anatomy / Trauma · Answer: C**

**Vignette Summary:** 45M twisted ankle, pain inferior to lateral malleolus → mechanism.

**The Discriminator:** Tenderness just distal to the lateral malleolus = anterior talofibular ligament injury, the most commonly sprained ankle ligament, caused by inversion.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** With the foot plantarflexed and inverted, body weight loads the lateral ankle ligaments (ATFL → CFL → PTFL in order of severity). Because the lateral ligamentous complex is weaker than the deltoid medially, inversion injuries dominate and produce tenderness over the ATFL just anteroinferior to the lateral malleolus. Eversion injuries (less common) damage the deltoid ligament medially.

**Cognitive Demand:** Identify the mechanism causing lateral ankle sprain

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Inversion injury → lateral ankle sprain → anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) is the most commonly injured, followed by calcaneofibular ligament. The mechanism is plantar flexion + inversion.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: Ottawa ankle rules. X-ray needed if point tenderness over posterior distal 6 cm of fibula or medial malleolus or base of 5th metatarsal, OR inability to bear weight. ATFL is most commonly injured. Treatment: RICE, early mobilization, PT. Or: grade I (stretch), II (partial tear), III (complete rupture).

**Variable Pivot:** If pain proximal to lateral malleolus + positive squeeze test + external rotation mechanism → high ankle (syndesmotic) sprain. If severe lateral ankle pain + inability to bear weight + ecchymosis → consider lateral malleolus fracture. If medial ankle injury + eversion mechanism → deltoid ligament sprain or medial malleolus fracture.

**Options:**

- **(A)** Dorsiflexion. High ankle (syndesmotic) sprain, tenderness above the joint line.
- **(B)** Eversion. Medial (deltoid) ligament injury, not lateral.
- **(C) ✅ CORRECT** Inversion. Mechanism for ATFL sprain.
- **(D)** Plantar flexion. Alone doesn't sprain ligaments; combines with inversion typically.

**Tid Bit:** ATFL is the most commonly torn ligament in the body. "Always Tears First Ligament."

## Q119 — Foreign body aspiration → V/Q mismatch

**Block 6 · Pulmonology / Pediatrics / Respiratory Physiology · Answer: E**

**Vignette Summary:** 3-year-old, sudden choking on candy, decreased left-sided breath sounds, SpO₂ 93%, CXR showing unilateral findings.

**The Discriminator:** Aspirated foreign body in a mainstem bronchus partially obstructs ventilation while perfusion continues → low V/Q (shunt-like) physiology → hypoxemia.

**Why the Answer is Correct:** A bronchial foreign body produces regional hypoventilation in the affected lung. Perfusion to that lung is largely preserved (hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction only partially compensates), so blood traverses poorly ventilated alveoli and returns under-oxygenated to the left heart. A V/Q mismatch with a low V/Q ratio. This drops PaO₂ and SpO₂. Diffusion and chest wall mechanics are intact.

**Cognitive Demand:** Identify the mechanism causing hypoxemia after foreign body aspiration into one main bronchus

**NBME Trap Fingerprint:** Foreign body into left main bronchus → left lung hypoventilated → V/Q mismatch. Ventilation decreases but perfusion continues → blood passes left lung unoxygenated → hypoxemia.

**Costume Change:** Reappears as: hypoxemia mechanisms. Hypoventilation (high CO2, low O2, normal A-a gradient), diffusion impairment (low DLCO, high A-a gradient, worsens with exercise), V/Q mismatch (most common cause of hypoxemia, high A-a gradient, responds to O2), intrapulmonary shunt (low V/Q areas, high A-a gradient, does NOT fully correct with O2), intracardiac shunt (does not correct with O2).

**Variable Pivot:** If hypoxemia + elevated PCO2 + normal A-a gradient → global hypoventilation (CNS, neuromuscular). If hypoxemia does not correct with supplemental O2 → intracardiac or intrapulmonary shunt. If hypoxemia at high altitude → low FiO2 → normal A-a gradient but low PO2. If foreign body identified → flexible bronchoscopy for retrieval under anesthesia.

**Options:**

- **(A)** Decreased chest wall compliance. Would cause restrictive physiology, not focal hypoxemia after aspiration.
- **(B)** Diffusion defect. Alveolar membrane is normal; foreign body doesn't thicken it.
- **(C)** Extrapulmonary right-to-left shunt. Congenital cardiac shunt; not implicated here.
- **(D)** Increased chest wall compliance. Would aid ventilation, not impair oxygenation.
- **(E) ✅ CORRECT** Ventilation-perfusion mismatch. Obstructed segment is perfused but not ventilated → hypoxemia.

**Tid Bit:** Right mainstem is the classic aspirated-FB site (straighter, wider); but any mainstem obstruction → low V/Q hypoxemia.
