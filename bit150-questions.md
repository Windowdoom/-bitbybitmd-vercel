## Q1. Hematology / Genetic

A 51-year-old woman stops menstruating and develops progressive fatigue. Her skin appears abnormally tanned despite lacking sun exposure. She has new-onset joint pain in her 2nd and 3rd metacarpophalangeal joints. Labs reveal an elevated fasting glucose and a transferrin saturation of 65%. What is the most appropriate initial therapy to prevent further end-organ damage?

- A. Intravenous deferoxamine infusion
- B. Oral penicillamine therapy
- C ✅. Serial removal of erythrocytes
- D. Dietary restriction of branched-chain amino acids
- E. Administration of ursodeoxycholic acid

**Answer: C.** Serial removal of erythrocytes

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Hereditary Hemochromatosis (HFE mutation). Pathophysiology: Due to a mutated HFE gene, enterocytes lose their ability to sense iron levels, leading to decreased hepcidin and massive unregulated intestinal iron absorption. Iron deposits in the skin (bronze), pancreas (diabetes), and joints (pseudogout/arthritis). Symptoms in women are delayed until menopause due to protective iron loss from menstruation. Why Correct: Serial phlebotomy (removal of erythrocytes) is the first-line treatment. It directly depletes the body's iron stores by forcing hematopoiesis. Pedagogical Takeaway: Menopause unveils hemochromatosis in women. Treat primary hemochromatosis with phlebotomy; reserve chelators (deferoxamine) for secondary iron overload (e.g., chronic transfusions).

## Q3. Pulmonology / Trauma

A 31-year-old woman is brought to the ED after a severe motor vehicle collision where she struck the steering wheel. She is hypotensive (80/50 mmHg), tachycardic, and has distant heart sounds. Her neck veins are markedly distended. During inspiration, her systolic blood pressure drops by 15 mmHg. What is the most appropriate next step in management?

- A. Emergent needle decompression of the left chest
- B. Administration of a 2L normal saline bolus
- C ✅. Echocardiography-guided pericardial drainage
- D. Immediate endotracheal intubation
- E. Placement of a resuscitative endovascular balloon

**Answer: C.** Echocardiography-guided pericardial drainage

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Cardiac Tamponade. Pathophysiology: Blunt chest trauma can cause bleeding into the pericardial sac. Beck's Triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds) plus pulsus paradoxus (>10mmHg SBP drop on inspiration) confirms the diagnosis. The fluid restricts right-sided heart filling, profoundly dropping cardiac output. Why Correct: Pericardiocentesis (drainage) is the immediate lifesaving intervention to relieve the pressure and restore venous return. Pedagogical Takeaway: Distinguish tamponade from tension pneumothorax: both have JVD and hypotension, but tamponade features muffled heart sounds and equal bilateral breath sounds.

## Q4. Microbiology / Gastroenterology

A 72-year-old man develops profuse, foul-smelling, watery diarrhea 10 days after completing a course of clindamycin for an aspiration pneumonia. His WBC count is elevated to 18, 000/mm³. Which of the following diagnostic tests is the most appropriate to confirm the etiology of his current symptoms?

- A. Stool culture on MacConkey agar
- B. Colonoscopy with random biopsies
- C ✅. Stool assay for bacterial exotoxins
- D. Blood cultures for anaerobic bacteremia
- E. Fecal leukocyte smear

**Answer: C.** Stool assay for bacterial exotoxins

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Clostridioides difficile Colitis. Pathophysiology: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins) disrupt normal colonic flora, allowing C. difficile spores to germinate. The bacteria secrete Toxin A (enterotoxin) and Toxin B (cytotoxin), destroying the cytoskeleton of enterocytes. Why Correct: The standard diagnostic test is a stool assay (EIA or PCR) detecting Toxins A/B. Stool cultures are useless because non-toxigenic C. diff exists in normal flora. Pedagogical Takeaway: Never culture for C. diff. Test for the toxin. First-line treatment is now Oral Vancomycin or Fidaxomicin.

## Q5. Renal / Nephrotic

A 58-year-old man presents with severe fatigue, back pain, and massive swelling in his lower extremities. Urinalysis reveals 4+ proteinuria and oval fat bodies. Serum albumin is 1.8 g/dL. A bone marrow biopsy demonstrates 15% clonal plasma cells. The pathogenesis of this patient's renal disease is most likely driven by the glomerular deposition of which substance?

- A. Misfolded transthyretin proteins
- B ✅. Immunoglobulin light chains
- C. IgA immune complexes
- D. Anti-glomerular basement membrane IgG
- E. Phospholipase A2 receptor antibodies

**Answer: B.** Immunoglobulin light chains

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Renal AL Amyloidosis (secondary to Multiple Myeloma). Pathophysiology: The patient has Multiple Myeloma (clonal plasma cells, back pain/bone lesions). Plasma cells overproduce immunoglobulin light chains, which misfold into beta-pleated sheets (amyloid). These deposit in the mesangium and capillary walls of the kidney. Why Correct: AL amyloidosis is directly caused by the deposition of monoclonal immunoglobulin light chains, disrupting the filtration barrier and causing nephrotic syndrome. Pedagogical Takeaway: Multiple myeloma is the most common cause of renal amyloidosis. Look for nephrotic syndrome + bone pain in an older adult.

## Q6. Endocrinology / GI

A 48-year-old man presents with severe, large-volume watery diarrhea (up to 8 liters a day), profound muscle weakness, and abdominal cramps. Labs show a potassium level of 1.9 mEq/L and achlorhydria. A CT scan of the abdomen reveals a 4-cm mass in the tail of the pancreas. What is the normal physiological function of the hormone being overproduced by this mass?

- A. Stimulates gastric acid secretion
- B. Inhibits insulin and glucagon release
- C ✅. Stimulates intestinal chloride and fluid secretion
- D. Promotes gallbladder contraction
- E. Induces glycogenolysis in the liver

**Answer: C.** Stimulates intestinal chloride and fluid secretion

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: VIPoma (WDHA Syndrome). Pathophysiology: VIPomas secrete Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide (VIP). WDHA stands for Watery Diarrhea, Hypokalemia, and Achlorhydria. VIP normally acts to relax GI smooth muscle and stimulate fluid/electrolyte secretion into the gut lumen. Why Correct: VIP stimulates intestinal chloride secretion (water follows), causing massive secretory diarrhea, which leads to the severe hypokalemia and muscle weakness seen here. Pedagogical Takeaway: Massive secretory diarrhea + hypokalemia + pancreatic mass = VIPoma. Contrast with Gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison), which causes peptic ulcers.

## Q7. Immunology / Hypersensitivity

A 32-year-old landscaper develops a severely pruritic, blistering, linear rash on his forearms 48 hours after clearing brush in a wooded area. Which of the following cells is the primary mediator of this localized inflammatory response?

- A. IgE-sensitized mast cells
- B. Complement-fixing neutrophils
- C ✅. Cytotoxic CD8+ T cells and Th1 macrophages
- D. Circulating immune complexes
- E. Eosinophils triggered by IL-5

**Answer: C.** Cytotoxic CD8+ T cells and Th1 macrophages

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Contact Dermatitis (Poison Ivy). Pathophysiology: Urushiol acts as a hapten, triggering a Type IV (Delayed) Hypersensitivity reaction. Upon re-exposure, sensitized CD4+ Th1 cells release IFN-gamma, activating macrophages, while CD8+ T cells directly induce apoptosis in skin cells, resulting in a blistering rash 2-3 days later. Why Correct: Type IV reactions are strictly cell-mediated (T-cells and macrophages), completely independent of antibodies. Pedagogical Takeaway: Linear vesicular rash from the woods = Poison Ivy. Type IV hypersensitivities take 48-72 hours to manifest (TB test, contact dermatitis, graft rejection).

## Q8. Neurology / Pharmacology

A 68-year-old man with a history of alcohol use disorder is brought to the ED with severe confusion, ataxia, and rhythmic back-and-forth eye movements. He is given an intravenous infusion of dextrose by paramedics before arriving. His neurologic status subsequently deteriorates. This patient's acute decompensation is due to the failure of an enzyme that requires which of the following as a cofactor?

- A. Pyridoxine
- B. Niacin
- C. Riboflavin
- D ✅. Thiamine
- E. Cobalamin

**Answer: D.** Thiamine

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Wernicke Encephalopathy precipitated by glucose administration. Pathophysiology: Chronic alcohol use leads to thiamine (Vitamin B1) deficiency. Thiamine is a required cofactor for key metabolic enzymes (Pyruvate dehydrogenase, Alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, Transketolase). Why Correct: Administering glucose forces cellular metabolism to run, rapidly consuming the last remaining traces of thiamine. This completely halts ATP production in the vulnerable mammillary bodies of the brain, precipitating or worsening Wernicke encephalopathy. Pedagogical Takeaway: Always give Thiamine BEFORE Dextrose in alcoholic or malnourished patients to prevent acute Wernicke encephalopathy.

## Q9. Pharmacology / Renal

A 55-year-old woman with a history of heart failure is started on an ACE inhibitor and a diuretic. Two weeks later, she presents with bilateral breast tenderness and laboratory testing reveals a potassium level of 5.8 mEq/L (normal: 3.5-5.0). Which diuretic was most likely prescribed?

- A. Furosemide
- B. Hydrochlorothiazide
- C ✅. Spironolactone
- D. Acetazolamide
- E. Chlorthalidone

**Answer: C.** Spironolactone

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Spironolactone side effects (Hyperkalemia and endocrine disruption). Pathophysiology: Spironolactone is an aldosterone receptor antagonist acting on the principal cells of the collecting duct. It prevents sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion, causing hyperkalemia. Because its steroid structure is similar to estrogen and androgens, it cross-reacts, causing gynecomastia in men and breast tenderness/irregular menses in women. Why Correct: Only potassium-sparing diuretics (like spironolactone) cause hyperkalemia, and spironolactone is notorious for its anti-androgenic/estrogenic side effects. Pedagogical Takeaway: ACE inhibitors also raise potassium. The combination of ACEi + Spironolactone requires careful potassium monitoring.

## Q10. Microbiology / Cardiovascular

A 21-year-old man with a history of intravenous drug use presents with high fever, chills, and a new-onset holosystolic murmur at the lower left sternal border that increases in intensity with inspiration. Blood cultures grow gram-positive cocci in clusters. What is the most appropriate empiric pharmacotherapy?

- A. Oral amoxicillin-clavulanate
- B. Intravenous ceftriaxone monotherapy
- C ✅. Intravenous vancomycin and gentamicin
- D. Intravenous penicillin G
- E. Intravenous metronidazole

**Answer: C.** Intravenous vancomycin and gentamicin

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Acute Right-Sided (Tricuspid) Infective Endocarditis. Pathophysiology: IV drug users inject skin flora directly into the venous system, seeding the first valve the blood hits: the tricuspid valve. Staphylococcus aureus (gram-positive cocci in clusters) is the classic culprit for acute, destructive endocarditis. The murmur increasing with inspiration (Carvallo's sign) confirms right-sided pathology. Why Correct: Empiric coverage must hit MRSA and gram-negatives pending final cultures. Vancomycin covers MRSA, and gentamicin provides synergistic bactericidal activity. Pedagogical Takeaway: IVDU = Tricuspid valve = S. aureus. Remember, right-sided murmurs increase with inspiration due to increased venous return.

## Q11. Cardiology / Shock

A 65-year-old man is brought to the ICU following a massive anterior myocardial infarction. His blood pressure is 75/45 mmHg, and his extremities are cold and clammy. Invasive hemodynamic monitoring is placed. Which of the following profiles is most expected in this patient? (CO = Cardiac Output, PCWP = Pulmonary Capillary Wedge Pressure, SVR = Systemic Vascular Resistance)

- A. Decreased CO, Decreased PCWP, Increased SVR
- B. Increased CO, Decreased PCWP, Decreased SVR
- C ✅. Decreased CO, Increased PCWP, Increased SVR
- D. Decreased CO, Normal PCWP, Decreased SVR
- E. Increased CO, Increased PCWP, Normal SVR

**Answer: C.** Decreased CO, Increased PCWP, Increased SVR

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Cardiogenic Shock. Pathophysiology: Massive MI destroys left ventricular pumping ability. Forward flow drops drastically (Decreased CO). Blood backs up into the left atrium and pulmonary circulation (Increased PCWP). The body attempts to compensate for the severe hypotension by massive sympathetic peripheral vasoconstriction (Increased SVR). Why Correct: Elevated PCWP is the defining feature that differentiates cardiogenic shock from hypovolemic shock (where PCWP would be low). Pedagogical Takeaway: Shock hemodynamics: Cardiogenic = High PCWP. Hypovolemic = Low PCWP. Septic = High CO / Low SVR (warm shock).

## Q12. Genetics / Hematology

A 28-year-old phenotypically normal woman has a brother with cystic fibrosis. Both of their parents are known carriers of the DeltaF508 mutation. The woman is pregnant with her first child. Assuming her partner is a carrier, what is the probability that the woman herself carries the mutated allele?

- A. 25%
- B. 33%
- C. 50%
- D ✅. 67%
- E. 75%

**Answer: D.** 67%

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Autosomal Recessive Carrier Probability. Pathophysiology: Cystic fibrosis is an autosomal recessive disease. When two carriers mate (Aa x Aa), the classic Punnett square yields: 25% AA (normal), 50% Aa (carrier), and 25% aa (affected). Why Correct: The key is that the woman is *phenotypically normal*. Therefore, we completely eliminate the 25% 'aa' possibility from the denominator. She must fall into the remaining 75%. Of that remaining pool, 2 out of 3 are carriers (Aa). Thus, her probability is 2/3, or ~67%. Pedagogical Takeaway: Always adjust the denominator when a patient's phenotype is known in genetics questions. If they do not have an AR disease, the denominator changes from 4 to 3.

## Q13. Neurology / Anatomy

A 24-year-old woman presents with double vision. When asked to look to her right, her right eye abducts normally (with horizontal nystagmus), but her left eye fails to adduct past the midline. Convergence is intact. A lesion in which of the following structures is responsible for this deficit?

- A. Left oculomotor nucleus
- B. Right abducens nucleus
- C ✅. Left medial longitudinal fasciculus
- D. Right medial longitudinal fasciculus
- E. Left paramedian pontine reticular formation

**Answer: C.** Left medial longitudinal fasciculus

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Internuclear Ophthalmoplegia (INO). Pathophysiology: The Medial Longitudinal Fasciculus (MLF) connects the CN VI nucleus (abducens, lateral gaze) to the contralateral CN III nucleus (oculomotor, medial gaze). When looking right, the right CN VI fires to abduct the right eye, and sends a signal via the LEFT MLF to tell the left eye to adduct. Why Correct: Because the left eye fails to adduct during conjugate gaze, the signal never reached the left CN III. The broken cable is the Left MLF. Intact convergence proves the medial rectus muscle and CN III nucleus themselves are working. Pedagogical Takeaway: The side of the INO (the eye that cannot adduct) is the side of the MLF lesion. Highly associated with Multiple Sclerosis in young adults.

## Q14. Pulmonology / Pathology

A 66-year-old man with a 45-pack-year smoking history presents with severe exertional dyspnea. Physical exam reveals a barrel chest, hyperresonance to percussion, and distant breath sounds. Pulmonary function testing demonstrates an FEV1/FVC ratio of 60% and a severely decreased DLCO. Which of the following mechanisms best explains the decreased DLCO in this patient?

- A. Thickening of the alveolar-capillary membrane
- B ✅. Destruction of alveolar septal walls
- C. Vascular occlusion by thromboemboli
- D. Smooth muscle hypertrophy in the bronchioles
- E. Excessive mucus production in large airways

**Answer: B.** Destruction of alveolar septal walls

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Centriacinar Emphysema. Pathophysiology: Chronic smoking triggers macrophages and neutrophils to release proteases (elastase), overwhelming endogenous alpha-1 antitrypsin. This causes the destruction of alveolar walls and capillary beds. Why Correct: DLCO (Diffusing Capacity of the Lungs for Carbon Monoxide) measures the surface area available for gas exchange. Destruction of alveolar septa physically removes the capillary beds and surface area, drastically lowering DLCO. Pedagogical Takeaway: Emphysema uniquely lowers DLCO among obstructive diseases. Chronic bronchitis and asthma typically have a normal or even increased DLCO.

## Q15. Biochemistry / Metabolism

A 4-year-old boy is brought to the clinic with severe fasting hypoglycemia, massive hepatomegaly, and hyperuricemia. Laboratory tests reveal elevated blood lactate levels. Administration of glucagon fails to increase his serum glucose. A deficiency in which of the following enzymes most likely explains this presentation?

- A. Acid alpha-glucosidase
- B. Debranching enzyme
- C ✅. Glucose-6-phosphatase
- D. Muscle glycogen phosphorylase
- E. Galactose-1-phosphate uridyltransferase

**Answer: C.** Glucose-6-phosphatase

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Von Gierke Disease (Type I Glycogen Storage Disease). Pathophysiology: Glucose-6-phosphatase is the final step in both glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis in the liver. Without it, the liver cannot release free glucose into the blood. Glucose-6-phosphate builds up, shunting into glycolysis (causing high lactate) and the pentose phosphate pathway (causing high uric acid). Why Correct: The classic triad of severe fasting hypoglycemia, hepatomegaly, and lactic acidosis points squarely to Von Gierke. Glucagon fails because the exit door (G6Pase) is padlocked. Pedagogical Takeaway: Lactic acidosis distinguishes Von Gierke (Type I) from Cori disease (Type III, debranching enzyme). Pompe (Type II) affects the heart, not the liver's glucose output.

## Q17. Renal / Vasculature

A 22-year-old woman is evaluated for severe hypertension (180/110 mmHg). She has no family history of early cardiovascular disease. MR angiography reveals a 'string of beads' appearance in her right renal artery. Laboratory evaluation will most likely reveal which of the following?

- A. Decreased plasma renin activity
- B ✅. Elevated serum aldosterone
- C. Elevated serum creatinine
- D. Decreased serum sodium
- E. Elevated urine metanephrines

**Answer: B.** Elevated serum aldosterone

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Fibromuscular Dysplasia (FMD). Pathophysiology: FMD is a non-atherosclerotic, non-inflammatory disease of blood vessels, characterized by medial fibroplasia ('string of beads' on imaging). It classically affects young women and narrows the renal artery. Why Correct: The narrowing decreases renal perfusion. ## Q18. Pharmacology / Autonomics

A 12-year-old boy with asthma requires acute treatment for a severe exacerbation. He is given an inhaled medication that results in rapid bronchodilation. A potential side effect of this medication involves tremors and tachycardia. The receptor targeted by this drug primarily exerts its effect through which of the following intracellular cascades?

- A. Inhibition of adenylyl cyclase
- B. Activation of phospholipase C
- C ✅. Stimulation of adenylyl cyclase
- D. Opening of ligand-gated ion channels
- E. Activation of a receptor tyrosine kinase

**Answer: C.** Stimulation of adenylyl cyclase

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Beta-2 Agonist mechanism (Albuterol). Pathophysiology: Albuterol stimulates beta-2 adrenergic receptors in bronchial smooth muscle. All beta receptors (1, 2, and 3) are Gs-protein coupled. Why Correct: Gs proteins stimulate adenylyl cyclase, converting ATP to cAMP. Beta 1, 2, 3 are all Gs (Stimulates Adenylyl Cyclase).

## Q19. GI / Hepatology

## Q21. Hematology / Thrombophilia

A 33-year-old man presents to the emergency department with sudden shortness of breath and a swollen, painful right leg following a 12-hour road trip. He has no past medical history, but notes his father died of a 'blood clot in the lungs' at age 40. Duplex ultrasound confirms a deep vein thrombosis. Which of the following is the most likely molecular mechanism underlying his condition?

- A. Deficiency of an inhibitor that targets thrombin and factor Xa
- B ✅. A mutant coagulation factor that resists cleavage by activated protein C
- C. A single point mutation in the 3' untranslated region of the prothrombin gene
- D. Impaired platelet glycoprotein IIb/IIIa function
- E. Decreased hepatic synthesis of protein S

**Answer: B.** A mutant coagulation factor that resists cleavage by activated protein C

**Explanation:** 01 The Pivot Point A young, otherwise healthy patient develops a DVT/PE provoked by stasis (road trip), coupled with a strong family history of early fatal venous thromboembolism. This immediately pivots us to an inherited thrombophilia. 02 The Master Discriminator The patient's demographic. In patients of European descent, the most common inherited cause of hypercoagulability is Factor V Leiden (accounting for ~5% of the population). 03 First Principles (Mechanism) Factor V is a critical cofactor in the coagulation cascade. Normally, Activated Protein C (APC) limits clot formation by cleaving and inactivating Factor Va. In Factor V Leiden, a point mutation alters the cleavage site. The mutant Factor V resists degradation by APC, leading to unchecked thrombin generation and a hypercoagulable state. 04 Distractor Logic A (Antithrombin deficiency): The Rarity Trap. This causes severe hypercoagulability (and heparin resistance), but it is significantly less common than Factor V Leiden. Always play the odds on USMLE unless a specific clue (like 'heparin didn't work') is given. C (Prothrombin G20210A): The Second-Place Trap. This is the second most common inherited thrombophilia, causing excess prothrombin production, but Factor V Leiden is the primary 'go-to' for generic familial DVT. D (Glanzmann thrombasthenia): The Bleeding Trap. Defects in GPIIb/IIIa impair platelet aggregation, leading to mucocutaneous BLEEDING, not thrombosis.

## Q22. Infectious Disease / Virology

A 26-year-old woman presents to urgent care with a 2-week history of persistent fever, severe sore throat, and night sweats. Physical examination reveals generalized non-tender lymphadenopathy involving the cervical, axillary, and inguinal chains. Laboratory testing shows a hemoglobin of 10.1 g/dL, a leukocyte count of 1, 800/mm³, and a platelet count of 65, 000/mm³. A heterophile antibody test is negative. What is the most likely diagnosis?

- A. Epstein-Barr virus infection
- B. Toxoplasma gondii infection
- C ✅. Acute HIV infection
- D. Secondary syphilis
- E. Cytomegalovirus infection

**Answer: C.** Acute HIV infection

**Explanation:** 01 The Pivot Point The patient presents with a 'mononucleosis-like' illness (fever, sore throat, generalized lymphadenopathy), but the heterophile antibody (Monospot) test is negative. 02 The Master Discriminator Profound Pancytopenia. A leukocyte count of 1, 800 and platelets of 65, 000 is far too severe for standard viral illnesses like CMV or heterophile-negative EBV. This degree of bone marrow suppression in the setting of generalized LAD points directly to a retrovirus. 03 First Principles (Mechanism) Acute Retroviral Syndrome (Acute HIV) typically presents 2-4 weeks post-exposure. The massive viral replication directly infects CD4+ cells and macrophages, inducing a massive cytokine storm and direct bone marrow suppression, leading to the classic triad of mono-like symptoms, generalized adenopathy, and pancytopenia. HIV antibodies may still be negative in this 'window period, ' requiring a viral load (RNA) test for confirmation. 04 Distractor Logic A (EBV): The Pattern-Match Trap. EBV is the most common cause of infectious mono, but it is heterophile-positive in adults and rarely causes severe pancytopenia. D (Secondary syphilis): The LAD Trap. Causes generalized lymphadenopathy and fever, but classically presents with a maculopapular rash on the palms/soles and does not cause severe pancytopenia. E (CMV): The Monospot Trap. CMV is the classic cause of heterophile-negative mono, but again, it lacks the severe bone marrow suppression seen in acute HIV.

## Q23. Renal / Nephrology

A 54-year-old woman is hospitalized for osteomyelitis and is being treated with intravenous nafcillin. Two weeks into her therapy, she develops a diffuse maculopapular rash and a low-grade fever (101°F). Routine labs reveal her serum creatinine has spiked from a baseline of 0.8 mg/dL to 3.4 mg/dL. Urinalysis demonstrates white blood cell casts. Which additional finding is most likely present in her urine?

- A. Dysmorphic red blood cells
- B ✅. Eosinophils
- C. Muddy brown granular casts
- D. Oval fat bodies
- E. Bence-Jones proteins

**Answer: B.** Eosinophils

**Explanation:** 01 The Pivot Point A patient develops Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) while receiving a beta-lactam antibiotic (nafcillin), accompanied by systemic allergic signs (fever, rash). 02 The Master Discriminator The triad of AKI + Fever + Rash 1-2 weeks after starting a drug is the classic signature of Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN). Pathophysiology: The urea cycle (liver mitochondria + cytoplasm) converts toxic ammonia to urea for excretion. Sequence: NH3 → carbamoyl phosphate (CPS I) → citrulline (OTC) → argininosuccinate (argininosuccinate synthetase) → arginine + fumarate (argininosuccinate LYASE) → urea + ornithine (arginase). Deficiency of argininosuccinate lyase → argininosuccinate accumulates → citrulline also elevated upstream. Elevated urine orotic acid distinguishes from CPS I/NAGS deficiency (orotic acid elevated in OTC deficiency and argininosuccinate lyase deficiency due to overflow of carbamoyl phosphate into pyrimidine synthesis). Low arginine = downstream product absent. Why Correct: The pattern of elevated citrulline + argininosuccinate + low arginine localizes the block to argininosuccinate lyase. Treatment: protein restriction + arginine supplementation (essential amino acid when this enzyme is absent) + sodium benzoate/phenylacetate (alternative nitrogen excretion pathways). Pedagogical Takeaway: Urea cycle quick localization: Elevated ammonia with NO orotic acid = CPS I or NAGS deficiency (early blocks, no carbamoyl phosphate overflow). Elevated ammonia + elevated orotic acid = OTC deficiency (most common urea cycle disorder, X-linked). Elevated citrulline = ASS or ASL deficiency. All present as neonatal hyperammonemia after first feeding (protein load). Emergency treatment: stop protein, give glucose (anabolic), and nitrogen scavengers.

## Q150. Reproductive / Gynecology

A 29-year-old woman presents with irregular menstrual cycles (every 35-60 days), difficulty losing weight despite exercise, acne, and excess facial hair. BMI is 31. On ultrasound, both ovaries have more than 12 small follicles peripherally arranged ('string of pearls'). Lab work shows LH:FSH ratio of 3:1, mildly elevated free testosterone, and fasting insulin 24 µU/mL. Which of the following pathophysiologic mechanisms is considered central to her condition?

- A. Primary ovarian failure with elevated FSH and estrogen deficiency
- B ✅. Insulin resistance causing elevated insulin levels, which stimulate ovarian androgen production and inhibit SHBG
- C. Hypothalamic amenorrhea from excess exercise and low body fat percentage
- D. Hyperprolactinemia inhibiting GnRH pulsatility and causing anovulation
- E. Primary adrenal androgen excess from 21-hydroxylase deficiency

**Answer: B.** Insulin resistance causing elevated insulin levels, which stimulate ovarian androgen production and inhibit SHBG

**Explanation:** Diagnosis: Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS), most common endocrine disorder in reproductive-age women. Pathophysiology: The central defect is insulin resistance → compensatory hyperinsulinemia → insulin acts on ovarian theca cells (via IGF-1 receptors) → ↑ LH-stimulated androgen production. Elevated androgens: (1) inhibit follicular maturation → anovulation → oligomenorrhea, (2) aromatize to estrogen in fat (unopposed estrogen → endometrial hyperplasia risk), (3) cause hirsutism and acne. Insulin also suppresses hepatic SHBG → more free testosterone. The elevated LH:FSH ratio (>2:1) results from abnormal GnRH pulsatility (increased LH pulse amplitude from tonic estrogen). The small peripheral follicles on ultrasound are arrested antral follicles (not cysts) that failed to develop due to androgen excess. Why Correct: Insulin resistance driving androgen excess is the central pathophysiology. Metformin (insulin sensitizer) is used in PCOS to improve insulin sensitivity, restore menstrual cycles, and reduce androgen levels. Pedagogical Takeaway: PCOS Rotterdam criteria (2 of 3): oligoovulation, clinical/biochemical hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. Management: weight loss + metformin (insulin resistance) + OCPs (regulate cycles, antiandrogen via SHBG increase). Clomiphene or letrozole for ovulation induction if fertility desired. Long-term risks: type 2 DM, cardiovascular disease, endometrial cancer (unopposed estrogen).
